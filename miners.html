ASIC Miners for Home Mining: Complete Hardware Guide

Choosing the right ASIC miner for a home setup is different from picking hardware for a warehouse operation. Home miners need to consider voltage compatibility (120V standard outlets vs 240V dedicated circuits), noise levels for residential environments, power consumption relative to your electricity rate, and physical size constraints. This catalog covers 84 miners across nine algorithms â€” SHA-256 for Bitcoin, Scrypt for Litecoin and Dogecoin, KHeavyHash for Kaspa, Etchash for Ethereum Classic, Equihash for Zcash, X11 for Dash, Eaglesong for Nervos (CKB), Blake2b-Sia for Siacoin, and Blake2s for Kadena.

Understanding Voltage Categories

120V Native miners plug into any standard US household outlet and are the simplest path to home mining. Models like the Goldshell Mini-DOGE series, IceRiver KS0 lineup, and iPollo V1 Mini run on normal circuits without any electrical modifications. 240V miners are industrial-grade ASIC machines like the Antminer S19 Pro, S21, L7, and KS5 series â€” they deliver maximum hashrate but require a dedicated 240V circuit similar to a dryer outlet. Loki Kit miners bridge this gap by allowing 240V machines to run on 120V power at reduced hashrate (~40% of rated capacity), using aftermarket firmware like LuxOS, VNish, or Braiins OS+. Lottery miners like the Bitaxe Ultra and NerdMiner are low-power devices for solo mining â€” extremely unlikely to find a block, but the payout is a full block reward if you do.
