💡 Why Remote Management Matters

Your ASIC miner doesn’t care that you’re at work, on vacation, or sleeping. When a miner freezes, overheats, or drops off the pool, every minute of downtime is lost revenue. Remote management lets you fix problems from your phone without driving home.

Here’s what you can do with a proper remote setup:

  • Power cycle a frozen miner — the most common first fix for ASIC issues
  • Check hashrate and temperatures from your phone at work
  • Switch pools or adjust settings through the miner’s web interface
  • Shut down miners on schedule — perfect for peak-rate electricity windows
  • Get alerts when a miner drops offline or temperatures spike
  • SSH into miners running custom firmware (LuxOS, Braiins OS, Vnish) for advanced control

Real talk: Most home miners don’t discover remote management until they’ve already lost days of mining time to a frozen rig they couldn’t restart. Don’t wait for that lesson. Set this up now.

🔐 VPN Remote Access — Tailscale & Headscale

The core problem: your miners are on your home network. When you leave the house, you can’t reach them. Traditional solutions like port forwarding punch holes in your firewall and expose your network to the internet. That’s a terrible idea, especially with equipment worth thousands of dollars behind it.

The modern solution is a mesh VPN — software that creates a private encrypted tunnel between your devices, no matter where they are. Your phone at work can talk to your miners at home as if they were on the same network. No open ports. No exposed IP addresses. No complicated configuration.

  • Works behind any router or firewall

Which should you pick? If you’re reading this page for the first time, start with Tailscale. It’s free, it works in minutes, and the free plan supports 100 devices — more than any home miner will ever need. Move to Headscale later if you want full self-hosting and zero third-party dependency.

How Tailscale Works (The Simple Version)

Tailscale creates a private network (called a “tailnet”) that follows you everywhere. Every device you install it on gets a unique 100.x.x.x IP address. These addresses work from anywhere in the world — your couch, the office, a hotel. Traffic goes directly between your devices (peer-to-peer), encrypted end-to-end. Tailscale’s coordination servers help your devices find each other. When a direct connection isn’t possible, traffic may relay through Tailscale’s DERP servers, but the content stays encrypted end-to-end — Tailscale cannot read your data.

Quick Setup: Tailscale on Your Phone + Home PC

  1. Go to tailscale.com/download and install on your home computer (the one on the same network as your miners)
  2. Sign in with Google, Microsoft, or GitHub — free account
  3. Install the Tailscale app on your phone (Android or iOS)
  4. Sign in with the same account
  5. Both devices now appear in your tailnet — they can reach each other from anywhere

But my miners can’t run Tailscale... Correct. ASIC miners run stripped-down Linux and you can’t install extra software on most of them. That’s where the Raspberry Pi subnet router comes in (next section). The Pi runs Tailscale and acts as a gateway to your entire mining network.

🍓 Raspberry Pi Subnet Router

Your ASIC miners can’t install Tailscale directly. They’re single-purpose machines with locked-down firmware. The solution is a subnet router — a device on your mining network that runs Tailscale and exposes your entire local network to your tailnet. Every device on your home network becomes reachable through it, including every miner.

Already have a laptop or desktop that stays on 24/7? You can skip the Raspberry Pi entirely. Install Tailscale on that computer, enable subnet routing (same commands below — just run them on your PC instead of a Pi), and you’re done. The Pi is for miners who don’t have an always-on home computer. If your laptop goes to sleep or gets shut down, you lose remote access — so only use this shortcut if the machine truly stays on around the clock.

What You Need

  • Raspberry Pi 4B (2GB+ RAM) — typically $35–55 (prices vary by market). Pi 3B+ works too but Pi 4 is more reliable for always-on duty.
  • MicroSD card (16GB+) — get a decent brand (SanDisk, Samsung). Don’t use that ancient 4GB card from 2012.
  • Ethernet cable — wired connection to your router. Do NOT rely on Wi-Fi for this. Your entire remote access chain depends on this staying connected.
  • USB-C power supply — official Pi power supply recommended. Underpowered Pi = random disconnects.
  • A case with passive cooling — a small heatsink or aluminum case keeps it running cool 24/7.

Setup Steps

How to find your network range: On your home PC, run ipconfig (Windows) or ifconfig (Mac/Linux). If your IP is 192.168.1.x, your range is 192.168.1.0/24. If it’s 192.168.0.x, use 192.168.0.0/24. If it’s 10.0.0.x, use 10.0.0.0/24.

After Setup

Now when you’re away from home with Tailscale running on your phone, you can open a browser and type your miner’s local IP address (like 192.168.1.50) and the miner’s web interface loads — just like being at home. You can also SSH into miners running custom firmware. The Pi acts as your always-on gateway.

Don’t forget: Your Pi needs to stay on 24/7, plugged into your router with ethernet. If the Pi loses power or internet, you lose remote access. Consider putting the Pi and your router on a small UPS battery backup (typically $40–60, prices vary) so a brief power flicker doesn’t kill your remote access chain.

🔌 240V Remote Power Control

When a miner freezes, the most common first fix is power cycling it — turning it off and back on. If you’re not home, you need a way to do this remotely. For 240V ASIC miners (S19 Pro, S19J Pro, KS5, L7, S21), there are three proven paths to remote power control. Every product listed here is available for purchase on eBay or Amazon with a real price and a real buy link.

The 80% Rule is Law. NEC code says continuous loads (your miners run 24/7) must not exceed 80% of the circuit rating. A 30A circuit maxes at 24A continuous (5,760W at 240V). A 20A circuit maxes at 16A. Exceeding this creates fire risk. Codes vary by jurisdiction — follow your local code and consult an electrician. See our Electrical Setup Guide for the full breakdown.

🟢 Setup 1: WiFi Pool Pump Timer + Metered PDU (~$150–180)

This is the budget path most home miners use. A WiFi pool pump timer is a 240V relay designed for hot tubs and pool pumps — but it works perfectly for miners because it’s rated for high-amperage 240V loads. It hardwires between your breaker and your outlet, giving you remote on/off control from your phone via a smartphone app, Alexa, or Google voice commands. Pair it with a metered PDU that distributes power to your miners and shows you the wattage draw.

How it works: The pool pump timer sits between the breaker and the outlet (hardwired by an electrician). It controls power to the entire circuit. The metered PDU plugs into the outlet and distributes power to your miners with an LCD display showing amps and watts. You control on/off from your phone. All miners on that circuit turn on/off together.

This depends entirely on each miner’s power draw. Max continuous load at 80% rule: 24A = 5,760W.

  • Antminer S19 Pro (3,250W): 1 per 30A circuit. Draws ~13.5A at 240V
  • Antminer S19J Pro (3,050W): 1 per 30A circuit. Draws ~12.7A at 240V
  • Antminer KS5 (3,400W): 1 per 30A circuit. Draws ~14.2A at 240V
  • Antminer L3++ (942W): Up to 5 per 30A circuit (4,710W total, ~19.6A)

Always do your own math. Check the label on YOUR miner for its actual wattage. Divide by your voltage (240 or 120) to get amps. Stay under 80% of your circuit breaker rating. When in doubt, ask an electrician. See our Electrical Setup Guide for the full breakdown.

🧠 Firmware, SSH & Safe Shutdown

Every ASIC miner has a built-in web interface for configuration and monitoring. With your VPN set up (Tailscale + Pi subnet router), you can access these from anywhere. Custom firmware takes it further with SSH access, web APIs, and fleet management tools.

Stock Firmware (Bitmain/Factory)

What you get out of the box. Access the miner’s IP in a browser to see hashrate, temperatures, fan speeds, pool settings, and basic controls. Limited but functional for remote monitoring through your VPN.

Custom Firmware — LuxOS, Braiins OS+, Vnish

All three support SSH access, web APIs for scripting, and fleet management tools. Each handles shutdown and restart differently — and getting it wrong can leave a miner stuck off with no remote recovery. See our Firmware Guide: Shutdown, Restart & Scheduling for the full comparison table and per-firmware details.

SSH Access Through Tailscale

With Tailscale + Pi subnet router running, you can SSH into miners running custom firmware from anywhere. On your phone, use Termius (iOS/Android). On your laptop, open a terminal:

📋 SSH Commands by Firmware — Copy & Paste Ready
# SSH into any miner running custom firmware $ ssh [email protected] # Braiins OS # Stop mining (fans keep running) root@antminer:~# /etc/init.d/bosminer stop # Start mining root@antminer:~# /etc/init.d/bosminer start # Restart mining daemon root@antminer:~# /etc/init.d/bosminer restart # LuxOS # Step 1: Get a session ID (required before curtail) root@antminer:~# echo '{"command": "logon"}' | nc 127.0.0.1 4028 # Step 2: Put miner to sleep (replace SESSION_ID with the value from Step 1) root@antminer:~# echo '{"command": "curtail", "parameter":"SESSION_ID,sleep"}' | nc 127.0.0.1 4028 # Step 3: Wake miner back up root@antminer:~# echo '{"command": "curtail", "parameter":"SESSION_ID,wakeup"}' | nc 127.0.0.1 4028 # Stock Bitmain L3++ (CGMiner) root@antminer:~# /etc/init.d/cgminer.sh stop root@antminer:~# /etc/init.d/cgminer.sh start

LuxOS curtail warning: A miner put into sleep mode via the curtail command will stay curtailed even if you reboot the machine or restore power. The only way to resume mining is to send the wakeup command. If the session ID has expired after a reboot, run the logon command again to get a fresh one, then send wakeup. This is confirmed in the official Luxor documentation.

Stock S19 series firmware has SSH disabled. You cannot SSH into a stock Antminer S19, S19 Pro, S19J Pro, or S19 XP. Bitmain locked SSH access on all S19-series stock firmware. You must install custom firmware (LuxOS, Braiins OS, or Vnish) to use SSH commands. The L3++ with stock firmware does allow SSH.

Safe Shutdown Methods — Don’t Just Yank the Plug

ASIC miners have no power button. Most miners have been yanking the plug since day one. It works — until it doesn’t. When you yank the power cord on a running Antminer, the fans stop instantly while the ASIC chips are still at 70–85°C. This thermal shock can weaken solder joints on hashboards over time, especially on S17 and older S19 series miners.

Method 1: SSH poweroff Command Best Practice

SSH into the miner and type poweroff. This stops the mining process, lets the fans keep running for a brief cooldown, then halts the system. Wait 2–3 minutes after the command for chips to cool, then cut power at the breaker or power control device.

📋 SSH Poweroff Commands
# Graceful shutdown via SSH (works on all Antminers) $ ssh [email protected] root@antminer:~# poweroff

After running poweroff, fans won’t stop immediately — they keep spinning on residual power. When the miner has cooled down, it’s safe to cut power. This is the method Bitmain recommends.

Method 2: Stop the Mining Daemon Only (Keeps Miner On) Best Practice

Stop just the mining process. The miner stays on, fans keep running, chips cool down completely — but it’s no longer mining or drawing full power. Perfect for scheduled solar cutoff times when you want to resume later.

📋 Stop/Start Mining Commands by Firmware
# Braiins OS # Stop mining (miner stays on, fans keep running) root@antminer:~# /etc/init.d/bosminer stop # Resume mining root@antminer:~# /etc/init.d/bosminer start # LuxOS # Get session ID first (required) root@antminer:~# echo '{"command": "logon"}' | nc 127.0.0.1 4028 # Put miner to sleep (replace SESSION_ID) root@antminer:~# echo '{"command": "curtail", "parameter":"SESSION_ID,sleep"}' | nc 127.0.0.1 4028 # Wake miner back up (replace SESSION_ID) root@antminer:~# echo '{"command": "curtail", "parameter":"SESSION_ID,wakeup"}' | nc 127.0.0.1 4028 # Stock Bitmain L3++ (CGMiner) root@antminer:~# /etc/init.d/cgminer.sh stop root@antminer:~# /etc/init.d/cgminer.sh start # Stock S19 Series # SSH is DISABLED on stock S19 firmware. # You cannot stop/start mining via SSH on a stock S19. # Install custom firmware (LuxOS, Braiins OS, or Vnish) for SSH access.

Note: The daemon stop/start isn’t always 100% reliable. If the miner doesn’t resume properly, a reboot command or power cycle will fix it. For LuxOS, a miner in sleep mode stays curtailed even after a reboot — you must send the wakeup command to resume mining.

Method 3: Web Interface Reboot Good

Log into your miner’s web dashboard at its local IP address. Navigate to System → Reboot. This performs a clean reboot — gentler than a hard power cut. Available on all Antminers with stock or custom firmware. Works from any browser on your network (or through Tailscale remotely).

Method 4: Firmware API Commands Good

LuxOS, Braiins OS, and Vnish all expose API endpoints for remote control. These can be scripted for automation — stop mining at 7am when peak rates kick in, start at 9pm when free power begins. See our Firmware Guide for API details on each firmware.

Method 5: Scheduled Power Cut (Pool Timer or PDU) Combo

Set your WiFi pool pump timer or switched PDU to cut power on a daily schedule matching your electricity rates. Example: power on at 9:00 PM (free power starts), power off at 6:55 AM (5-minute buffer before peak rates at 7:00 AM).

Best practice: Combine methods — use a cron job or script to SSH poweroff 5 minutes before the timer cuts power. Graceful shutdown followed by a hard cutoff as a safety net.

Method 6: Circuit Breaker OK

Walk to your breaker panel and flip the breaker off. Cleaner than yanking a plug because the PSU handles a breaker trip more gracefully than a sudden cord disconnect. Still a hard cut — use SSH shutdown first if possible.

Method 7: Just Pull the Plug Last Resort

Yes, it works. Millions of miners do it daily. Modern PSUs have overcurrent protection. But if you have the option to do any of the above first, take 30 seconds and do it right. Your hashboards will thank you.

APW12 PSU note (S19 series): The APW12 has two power input lines. When plugging in: outer line first, then inner. When unplugging: inner line first, then outer. Getting this backwards can damage the PSU. Best practice (community guidance) — check your specific PSU manual for the manufacturer’s recommended sequence.

Recommended Shutdown Routine for Solar Miners

The ideal daily shutdown for peak-rate avoidance:

1. 6:50 AM — Automated script sends SSH poweroff to all miners (or stop daemon command if you want fans to cool)

2. 6:55 AM — WiFi pool pump timer or switched PDU cuts power to miner outlets (hard cutoff safety net, 5 min after graceful shutdown)

3. 9:00 PM — Timer or PDU restores power. Miners boot automatically and resume mining

This gives you a clean graceful shutdown every morning, a hard power cut as backup, and automatic restart every night. Total cost: one Raspberry Pi running a cron job + one WiFi pool pump timer (~$60). Set it once, forget it.

🚨 The Power-On Problem: No SSH command, firmware API, or software trick can turn a miner back on after a full shutdown. Once the OS is off, the network stack is dead. The ONLY way to restore power remotely is a pool pump timer, switched PDU, or smart relay that physically cuts and restores electricity to the miner. This is why every serious remote setup includes a remote power control device.

LuxOS SSH shutdown bug: LuxOS poweroff via SSH doesn’t always restart the miner properly — it may shut down and not come back up when power is restored. If you’re running LuxOS, rely on a hard power cycle (pool pump timer or PDU) for restarts rather than SSH shutdown.

DIY AutoPing — Scripted Automatic Restarts

No 30A/240V switched PDU available at retail for home miners includes built-in AutoPing. But you can build the same functionality with a simple script on your Raspberry Pi. The Pi pings each miner every 60 seconds. If a miner fails to respond after several consecutive attempts, the script triggers a power cycle through your Altair Virgo’s HTTP API, your Shelly’s REST API, or your pool pump timer’s app.

📋 Simple AutoPing Script (Bash) — Runs on Your Pi
#!/bin/bash # Simple autoping script - checks if miner responds, reboots if not # Run via cron: */5 * * * * /home/pi/autoping.sh MINER_IP="192.168.1.50" FAIL_FILE="/tmp/miner_fails" # Ping the miner if ! ping -c 3 -W 5 "$MINER_IP" &> /dev/null; then FAILS=$(cat "$FAIL_FILE" 2>/dev/null || echo 0) FAILS=$((FAILS + 1)) echo "$FAILS" > "$FAIL_FILE" # After 3 consecutive failures (15 min), trigger power cycle if [ "$FAILS" -ge 3 ]; then # Example: Shelly Pro 1PM relay toggle (off, wait, on) curl -s "http://192.168.1.100/relay/0?turn=off" sleep 10 curl -s "http://192.168.1.100/relay/0?turn=on" echo 0 > "$FAIL_FILE" # Optional: send yourself a notification logger "AutoPing: Rebooted miner at $MINER_IP" fi else echo 0 > "$FAIL_FILE" fi

How AutoPing works: You assign each miner an IP to ping. The script runs every 5 minutes via cron. If a miner fails to respond to 3 consecutive checks (15 minutes of downtime), the script triggers a power cycle through your Shelly relay, Altair Virgo HTTP API, or any other network-controllable power device. The miner restarts, reconnects to the pool, and resumes hashing — automatically, 24/7, whether you’re home or not.

🕑 Running Miners Only During Free Hours

If your utility gives you free electricity from 9 PM to 6 AM and your solar covers daytime, your miners can run on nearly zero electricity cost. The trick is automating the on/off schedule so you don’t have to do it manually every day.

There are three ways to automate this, depending on your hardware:

Option 1: WiFi Pool Pump Timer or PDU Scheduling

The simplest method. Your WiFi pool pump timer (Dewenwils) or Altair Virgo switched PDU has built-in scheduling through its app or web interface. No extra software needed.

  1. Open the timer’s smartphone app or PDU’s web interface (locally or through Tailscale)
  2. Set the schedule: ON at the start of your free/cheap window (e.g., 9:00 PM)
  3. Set OFF before peak rates kick in (e.g., 6:45 AM — give miners 15 minutes before peak starts at 7:00 AM)
  4. If using a PDU with a clock, verify NTP is enabled and the timezone is correct — a drifted clock means your schedule fires at the wrong time

Option 2: Firmware Power Profiles

Custom firmware can throttle or pause mining without cutting power to the machine. This is gentler on hardware than a hard power cut.

  • Braiins OS+: Power target mode lets you set a wattage limit. Schedule it through the API — set power target to 0W during peak hours and back to full during free hours. The miner stays on but stops hashing.
  • LuxOS: Supports curtailment profiles that can be triggered on a schedule or via API. Set a “sleep” profile for peak hours and a “full power” profile for free hours.
  • VNish: Supports scheduled frequency adjustments. Reduce frequency to minimum during expensive hours.

Why firmware scheduling beats hard power cuts: Repeatedly cutting and restoring power to an ASIC is harder on the machine than letting it idle. Firmware-based scheduling keeps the control board running and avoids the thermal shock of cold starts. If your setup supports both scheduling methods, use the firmware method for daily on/off and keep the hard power cut as a backup safety net.

Option 3: Shelly + Home Assistant

If you use Shelly DIN rail relays for power control, you can build automations in Home Assistant that trigger on time-of-use rate changes:

  • Create a time-based automation: IF time is 9:00 PM THEN turn on Shelly relay for mining circuit
  • Create the reverse: IF time is 6:45 AM THEN turn off Shelly relay
  • Add a condition: IF miner temperature sensor reads above 95°F THEN delay start until it cools (useful in Texas summers)

Home Assistant is free and open source, but it requires a dedicated device (Raspberry Pi or old PC) running 24/7 to execute automations.

Check your rate plan details. “Free nights” plans vary by provider. Some start at 9 PM, some at 8 PM. Some end at 6 AM, others at 5 AM. Get the exact hours from your utility and set your schedule accordingly. Running even one hour into peak rate can eat your savings.

🛡️ Security Best Practices

Your miners represent thousands of dollars of hardware generating real income. A poorly secured remote access setup is an invitation for someone to redirect your hashrate, steal your coins, or just brick your equipment for fun.

  • Avoid port forwarding. Port forwarding exposes your miner’s web interface directly to the internet and greatly increases your attack surface. If you must use it, lock it down with VPN, IP allowlists, and strong authentication. Tailscale is the safer path — zero open ports.
  • Change default passwords. Every Bitmain miner ships with username root and password root. Change this immediately. Custom firmware lets you set a proper password.
  • Use HTTPS when available. The Altair Virgo supports HTTPS. Braiins OS supports SSH with key-based authentication. Don’t use unencrypted HTTP over the internet.
  • Keep firmware updated. Custom firmware providers regularly patch security vulnerabilities. Running outdated firmware is running known exploits.
  • Separate your mining network. If your router supports VLANs, put your miners on a separate network segment from your family’s devices. A compromised miner shouldn’t be able to reach your personal laptop.
  • Monitor for pool changes. A common attack redirects your miner to a different pool/wallet. Periodically verify your pool settings haven’t been changed.
  • Tailscale key expiry. Tailscale auth keys expire (default is 180 days). Set a calendar reminder to re-authenticate your Pi before the key expires, or you’ll lose remote access until you can physically reach the Pi.

Headscale advantage: If you self-host with Headscale, your coordination server data never touches a third party. You control the authentication, the keys, and the routing. For miners with significant hashrate, this level of control can be worth the extra setup complexity.

🔧 Common Problems & Fixes

Things break. Here are the problems home miners run into most often with remote management, and how to fix them fast.

⚠️ “I lost Tailscale access to my Pi”

Tailscale auth keys expire after 180 days by default. Once expired, you need physical access to the Pi to re-authenticate. Fix: log into the Tailscale admin console, find the device, and disable key expiry. Set a calendar reminder 30 days before expiry if you prefer to keep it enabled.

⚠️ “My power schedule didn’t fire”

If using a WiFi pool pump timer, check your phone app — the timer may have lost WiFi connection after a router reboot. If using a PDU with built-in scheduling, check its internal clock — a power outage may have reset it. Verify NTP (network time sync) is enabled and the timezone is set correctly.

⚠️ “I can’t SSH into my miner”

Three things to check: (1) The miner’s IP may have changed — check your router’s DHCP lease list or set a static IP. (2) Stock Bitmain firmware locks SSH — verify custom firmware is still installed. (3) A firmware update or factory reset may have reverted the miner to stock, which kills SSH access.

⚠️ “My Pi lost its IP address”

Your router assigned a new IP via DHCP after a reboot or lease expiry. Fix: log into your router, find the Pi’s MAC address (run ip link show eth0 on the Pi), and create a DHCP reservation so it always gets the same IP. Tailscale access still works even if the local IP changes — it uses its own addresses.

⚠️ “Miner shows hashing but pool shows zero”

The miner thinks it’s working but shares aren’t reaching the pool. Check your internet connection first. If SSH is available, try pinging the pool address from the miner. Verify the pool URL and port haven’t changed — some pools retire old stratum addresses. Check that your firewall or router isn’t blocking outbound traffic on the pool’s port.

⚠️ “Metered PDU says miner is drawing 0 watts but it’s running”

The PDU’s current sensor isn’t reading the load. Either the current clamp isn’t seated properly on the cable, or the miner is plugged into a different outlet than the one you’re monitoring. This requires physical verification — no remote fix.
