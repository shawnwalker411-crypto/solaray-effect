How Tailscale Works (The Simple Version)

Tailscale creates a private network (called a "tailnet") that follows you everywhere. Every device you install it on gets a unique 100.x.x.x IP address. These addresses work from anywhere in the world — your couch, the office, a hotel. Traffic goes directly between your devices (peer-to-peer), encrypted end-to-end. Tailscale's servers only help your devices find each other — they never see your data.

Quick Setup: Tailscale on Your Phone + Home PC

  1. Go to tailscale.com/download and install on your home computer (the one on the same network as your miners)
  2. Sign in with Google, Microsoft, or GitHub — free account
  3. Install the Tailscale app on your phone (Android or iOS)
  4. Sign in with the same account
  5. Both devices now appear in your tailnet — they can reach each other from anywhere

But my miners can't run Tailscale... Correct. ASIC miners run stripped-down Linux and you can't install extra software on most of them. That's where the Raspberry Pi subnet router comes in (next section). The Pi runs Tailscale and acts as a gateway to your entire mining network.

🍓 Raspberry Pi Subnet Router

Your ASIC miners can't install Tailscale directly. They're single-purpose machines with locked-down firmware. The solution is a subnet router — a small computer on your mining network that runs Tailscale and exposes your entire local network to your tailnet. Every device on your home network becomes reachable through the Pi, including every miner.

What You Need

  • Raspberry Pi 4B (2GB+ RAM) — $35-55, widely available. Pi 3B+ works too but Pi 4 is more reliable for always-on duty
  • MicroSD card (16GB+) — get a decent brand (SanDisk, Samsung). Don’t use that ancient 4GB card from 2012
  • Ethernet cable — wired connection to your router. Do NOT rely on Wi-Fi for this. Your entire remote access chain depends on this staying connected
  • USB-C power supply — official Pi power supply recommended. Underpowered Pi = random disconnects
  • A case with passive cooling — a small heatsink or aluminum case keeps it running cool 24/7

Setup Steps

📋 Pi Subnet Router Setup Commands — Copy & Paste Ready
# 1. Flash Raspberry Pi OS Lite (no desktop needed) to your SD card # Use Raspberry Pi Imager - enable SSH in the settings before flashing # 2. Boot the Pi, connect via SSH from your home computer $ ssh [email protected] # 3. Update the system $ sudo apt update && sudo apt upgrade -y # 4. Install Tailscale $ curl -fsSL https://tailscale.com/install.sh | sh # 5. How to find your network range: On your home PC, run ipconfig (Windows) or ifconfig (Mac/Linux). If your IP is 192.168.1.x, your range is 192.168.1.0/24. If it's 192.168.0.x, use 192.168.0.0/24. If it's 10.0.0.x, use 10.0.0.0/24.

After Setup

Now when you're away from home with Tailscale running on your phone, you can open a browser and type your miner's local IP address (like 192.168.1.50) and the miner's web interface loads — just like being at home. You can also SSH into miners running custom firmware. The Pi acts as your always-on gateway.

Don’t forget: Your Pi needs to stay on 24/7, plugged into your router with ethernet. If the Pi loses power or internet, you lose remote access. Consider putting the Pi and your router on a small UPS battery backup ($40-60) so a brief power flicker doesn’t kill your remote access chain.

🔋 Smart PDUs for Mining — 30A, 40A & 240V

A smart PDU (Power Distribution Unit) is a power strip on steroids. It distributes power from one high-amperage circuit to multiple outlets — and the "smart" part means you can monitor power draw, switch outlets on/off remotely, and set up automatic power cycling. For miners, this is the single most valuable piece of remote management hardware you can buy.

A standard metered PDU shows you watts and amps on a screen. A smart PDU connects to your network and lets you do all of that from your phone — plus automatically reboot a miner when it stops responding.

What to Look For in a Mining PDU

  • Voltage rating: 240V for most ASIC miners. 120V only for small miners (Goldshell Mini series, lottery miners)
  • Amperage: 30A is the sweet spot for home mining (7,200W max / 5,760W at 80% rule). 40A+ exists for bigger setups
  • Plug type: L6-30P for 30A/240V circuits (the locking plug that goes into an L6-30R outlet)
  • Outlets: C13 outlets for most miners (S19 series, L3++, KS5). C19 outlets for newer high-power miners (S21, S21+)
  • Network connectivity: Ethernet (RJ45), Wi-Fi, or both — required for remote monitoring
  • Outlet-level switching: ability to turn individual outlets on/off remotely
  • AutoPing: automatically power cycle an outlet when the connected miner stops responding to pings
  • Power metering: per-outlet wattage, amps, kWh tracking

The 80% Rule is Law. NEC code says continuous loads (your miners run 24/7) must not exceed 80% of the circuit rating. A 30A circuit maxes at 24A continuous. A 20A circuit maxes at 16A. Exceeding this creates fire risk. No exceptions.

⚡ Safe Shutdown Methods — Don't Just Yank the Plug

ASIC miners have no power button. Most miners have been yanking the plug since day one. It works — until it doesn't. Here's why it matters and what to do instead.

Why Pulling the Plug Is Risky

When you yank the power cord on a running Antminer, the fans stop instantly. The ASIC chips inside were just running at 70-85°C. With zero airflow, those chips briefly spike even hotter before the aluminum heatsinks and housing can passively dissipate the heat. On S17 and S19 series miners, this thermal shock can weaken solder joints on hashboards over time. One hard yank probably won't kill anything. Doing it twice a day for a year — especially on older or overclocked hardware — accelerates hashboard failures.

S17 series warning: The Antminer S17 is already notorious for hashboard failures. If you're running an S17, never yank the plug while mining at full power. Always use a graceful shutdown method. These boards are fragile enough without thermal shock.

The Shutdown Methods — Gentlest to Most Aggressive

Method 1: SSH poweroff Command Best Practice

SSH into the miner and type poweroff. This stops the mining process, lets the fans keep running for a brief cooldown, then halts the system. Wait 2-3 minutes after the command for chips to cool, then cut power at the breaker or PDU.

📋 SSH Poweroff Commands
# Graceful shutdown via SSH (works on all Antminers) $ ssh [email protected] root@antminer:~# poweroff

After running poweroff, fans won't stop immediately — they keep spinning on residual power. When the miner has cooled down (hashboards no longer blowing hot air), it's safe to cut power. This is the method Bitmain recommends.

Method 2: Stop the Mining Daemon Only (Keeps Miner On) Best Practice

Stop just the mining process. The miner stays on, fans keep running, chips cool down completely — but it's no longer mining or drawing full power. Perfect for scheduled solar cutoff times when you want to resume later.

📋 Stop/Start Daemon Commands by Firmware
# Stop mining on stock Antminer firmware (S19 series) root@antminer:~# /etc/init.d/bmminer.sh stop # Stop mining on L3++ (stock firmware) root@antminer:~# /etc/init.d/cgminer.sh stop # Stop mining on Braiins OS root@antminer:~# /etc/init.d/bosminer stop # Resume mining later root@antminer:~# /etc/init.d/bosminer start

Note: The daemon stop/start isn't always 100% reliable. If the miner doesn't resume properly, a reboot command or power cycle will fix it.

Method 3: Web Interface Reboot Good

Log into your miner's web dashboard at its local IP address. Navigate to System → Reboot. This performs a clean reboot — it doesn't fully power off the miner, but it's a clean restart that's gentler than a hard power cut.

Available on all Antminers with stock or custom firmware. Works from any browser on your network (or through Tailscale remotely). No SSH knowledge needed.

Method 4: Firmware API Commands Good

LuxOS, Braiins OS, and Vnish all expose API endpoints for remote control. These can be scripted for automation — stop mining at 7am when peak rates kick in, start at 9pm when free power begins.

See our Firmware Guide for API details on each firmware. LuxOS has 45+ API commands including pause/resume mining.

Method 5: Smart PDU Scheduled Power Cut Combo

Set your smart PDU (Synaccess SynLink, Altair Virgo, etc.) to cut power on a daily schedule matching your electricity rates. For Sola Ray Effect's setup: power on at 9:00 PM (free power starts), power off at 6:50 AM (10-minute buffer before peak rates at 7:00 AM).

Best practice: Combine methods — use a cron job or script to SSH poweroff 5 minutes before the PDU cuts power. Graceful shutdown followed by a hard cutoff as a safety net.

Method 6: Circuit Breaker OK

Walk to your breaker panel and flip the breaker off. Cleaner than yanking a plug because the PSU handles a breaker trip more gracefully than a sudden cord disconnect (no strain on connectors, no arc at the plug). Still a hard cut — use SSH shutdown first if possible.

Method 7: Just Pull the Plug Last Resort

Yes, it works. Millions of miners do it daily. Modern PSUs have overcurrent protection. But if you have the option to do any of the above first, take 30 seconds and do it right. Your hashboards will thank you.

APW12 PSU note (S19 series): The APW12 has two power input lines. When plugging in: outer line first, then inner. When unplugging: inner line first, then outer. Getting this backwards can damage the PSU. This is from Bitmain's own documentation.

Recommended Shutdown Routine for Solar Miners

The ideal daily shutdown for peak-rate avoidance:

1. 6:50 AM — Automated script sends SSH poweroff to all miners (or stop daemon command if you want fans to cool)

2. 6:55 AM — Smart PDU cuts power to miner outlets (hard cutoff safety net, 5 min after graceful shutdown)

3. 9:00 PM — Smart PDU restores power. Miners boot automatically and resume mining

This gives you a clean graceful shutdown every morning, a hard power cut as backup, and automatic restart every night. Total cost: one Raspberry Pi running a cron job + one smart PDU. Set it once, forget it.

LuxOS SSH shutdown bug: As noted in the firmware section, LuxOS poweroff via SSH doesn't always restart the miner properly — it may shut down and not come back up when power is restored. If you're running LuxOS, rely on the smart PDU for power cycling rather than SSH shutdown. The PDU hard-cuts power, and the miner boots fresh when power returns.

The AutoPing Power Cycle (The Killer Feature)

This is the single most important automation for home miners. Here's how it works on a smart PDU like the Synaccess SynLink:

  1. You assign each outlet a "ping target" — the IP address of the miner plugged into that outlet
  2. The PDU sends a ping to each miner every 30-60 seconds
  3. If a miner fails to respond to X consecutive pings (you set the threshold), the PDU automatically power cycles that outlet
  4. The miner restarts, reconnects to the pool, and resumes hashing
  5. Optionally, the PDU sends you an email: "Outlet 3 was power cycled at 2:47 AM due to ping failure"

This happens automatically, 24/7, whether you’re home or not. It’s the closest thing to a “set and forget” mining operation.

🕑 Running Miners Only During Free Hours

If your utility gives you free electricity from 9 PM to 6 AM and your solar covers daytime, your miners can run on nearly zero electricity cost. The trick is automating the on/off schedule so you don’t have to do it manually every day.

There are three ways to automate this, depending on your hardware:

Option 1: Smart PDU Scheduling

Most smart PDUs (Synaccess SynLink, Tripp Lite, APC) have built-in outlet scheduling. This is the simplest method — no extra software needed.

  1. Log into your PDU’s web interface (locally or through Tailscale)
  2. Navigate to the outlet schedule or timer settings
  3. Set each mining outlet to ON at the start of your free/cheap window (e.g., 9:00 PM)
  4. Set each outlet to OFF before peak rates kick in (e.g., 5:45 AM — give miners 15 minutes to shut down gracefully)
  5. Verify the PDU’s internal clock is synced to NTP — a drifted clock means your schedule fires at the wrong time

Option 2: Firmware Power Profiles

Custom firmware can throttle or pause mining without cutting power to the machine. This is gentler on hardware than a hard power cut.

  • Braiins OS+: Power target mode lets you set a wattage limit. You can schedule it through the API — set power target to 0W during peak hours and back to full during free hours. The miner stays on but stops hashing.
  • LuxOS: Supports curtailment profiles that can be triggered on a schedule or via API. Set a “sleep” profile for peak hours and a “full power” profile for free hours.
  • VNish: Supports scheduled frequency adjustments. Reduce frequency to minimum during expensive hours.

Why firmware scheduling beats hard power cuts: Repeatedly cutting and restoring power to an ASIC is harder on the machine than letting it idle. Firmware-based scheduling keeps the control board running and avoids the thermal shock of cold starts. If your PDU supports scheduling AND you run custom firmware, use the firmware method.

Option 3: Shelly + Home Assistant

If you use Shelly smart switches for power control, you can build automations in Home Assistant that trigger on time-of-use rate changes:

  • Create a time-based automation: IF time is 9:00 PM THEN turn on Shelly relay for mining circuit
  • Create the reverse: IF time is 5:45 AM THEN turn off Shelly relay
  • Add a condition: IF miner temperature sensor reads above 95°F THEN delay start until it cools (useful in Texas summers)

Home Assistant is free and open source, but it requires a dedicated device (Raspberry Pi or old PC) running 24/7 to execute automations.

Check your rate plan details. “Free nights” plans vary by provider. Some start at 9 PM, some at 8 PM. Some end at 6 AM, others at 5 AM. Get the exact hours from your utility and set your schedule accordingly. Running even one hour into peak rate can eat your savings.

🛡️ Security Best Practices

Your miners represent thousands of dollars of hardware generating real income. A poorly secured remote access setup is an invitation for someone to redirect your hashrate, steal your coins, or just brick your equipment for fun.

  • Never use port forwarding. This exposes your miner's web interface directly to the internet. Anyone who finds it can access it. Use Tailscale instead — zero open ports.
  • Change default passwords. Every Bitmain miner ships with username root and password root. Change this immediately. Custom firmware lets you set a proper password.
  • Use HTTPS when available. Synaccess SynLink PDUs support HTTPS. Braiins OS supports SSH with key-based authentication. Don't use unencrypted HTTP over the internet.
  • Keep firmware updated. Custom firmware providers regularly patch security vulnerabilities. Running outdated firmware is running known exploits.
  • Separate your mining network. If your router supports VLANs, put your miners on a separate network segment from your family's devices. A compromised miner shouldn't be able to reach your personal laptop.
  • Monitor for pool changes. A common attack redirects your miner to a different pool/wallet. Periodically verify your pool settings haven't been changed.
  • Tailscale key expiry. Tailscale auth keys expire (default is 180 days). Set a calendar reminder to re-authenticate your Pi before the key expires, or you'll lose remote access until you can physically reach the Pi.

Headscale advantage: If you self-host with Headscale, your coordination server data never touches a third party. You control the authentication, the keys, and the routing. For miners with significant hashrate, this level of control can be worth the extra setup complexity.

🔧 Common Problems & Fixes

Things break. Here are the problems home miners run into most often with remote management, and how to fix them fast.

⚠️ “I lost Tailscale access to my Pi”

Tailscale auth keys expire after 180 days by default. Once expired, you need physical access to the Pi to re-authenticate. Fix: log into the Tailscale admin console, find the device, and disable key expiry. Set a calendar reminder 30 days before expiry if you prefer to keep it enabled.

⚠️ “My PDU schedule didn’t fire”

Check the PDU’s internal clock — a power outage may have reset it. Verify NTP (network time sync) is enabled and the timezone is set correctly. Some PDUs default to UTC, which will fire schedules hours off from your local time.

⚠️ “I can’t SSH into my miner”

Three things to check: (1) The miner’s IP may have changed — check your router’s DHCP lease list or set a static IP. (2) Stock Bitmain firmware locks SSH — verify custom firmware is still installed. (3) A firmware update or factory reset may have reverted the miner to stock, which kills SSH access.

⚠️ “My Pi lost its IP address”

Your router assigned a new IP via DHCP after a reboot or lease expiry. Fix: log into your router, find the Pi’s MAC address (run ip link show eth0 on the Pi), and create a DHCP reservation so it always gets the same IP. Tailscale access still works even if the local IP changes — it uses its own addresses.

⚠️ “Miner shows hashing but pool shows zero”

The miner thinks it’s working but shares aren’t reaching the pool. Check your internet connection first. If SSH is available, try pinging the pool address from the miner. Verify the pool URL and port haven’t changed — some pools retire old stratum addresses. Check that your firewall or router isn’t blocking outbound traffic on the pool’s port.

⚠️ “PDU says miner is drawing 0 watts but it’s running”

The PDU’s current sensor isn’t reading the load. Either the current clamp isn’t seated properly on the cable, or the miner is plugged into a different outlet than the one you’re monitoring. This requires physical verification — no remote fix.
